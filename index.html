Communicable Disease Review

Typhoid Fever
(Enteric Fever)

"Fecal‑oral transmission – prolonged fever, relative bradycardia, rose spots."

Epidemiology & Overview

Typhoid fever – acute systemic infection caused by Salmonella enterica serovar Typhi (Salmonella typhi).

Public Health Significance

  • Endemic in the Philippines; outbreaks occur with unsafe water/food.
  • Incubation period: 1‑3 weeks (average 2 weeks).
  • Case fatality rate without treatment: ~10%; with antibiotics <1%.
  • Chronic carriers (especially gallbladder) – major reservoir.

Agent & Reservoir

Etiologic agent: Salmonella Typhi (gram‑negative rod).

Reservoir: Humans only – patients and chronic carriers (especially women, elderly, gallstone patients).

Infectious dose: high (10⁵‑10⁹ organisms).

Clinical Features & Diagnosis

Signs & Symptoms

  • Step‑ladder fever (slowly rising, then sustained high fever).
  • Relative bradycardia (pulse is slow relative to temperature).
  • Headache, malaise, anorexia, abdominal pain.
  • Rose spots – faint, salmon‑colored maculopapular rash on trunk (in 30% of light‑skinned patients).
  • Constipation early, diarrhea later (pea‑soup stools).
  • Hepatosplenomegaly.

Diagnosis

  • Blood culture – gold standard (positive in first week).
  • Stool culture – positive after first week.
  • Bone marrow culture – most sensitive.
  • Widal test – detects antibodies (O and H antigens); limited specificity.
  • PCR (research).

Transmission & Carriers

Mode of Transmission

  • Fecal‑oral route (contaminated water, food, or hands).
  • Flies can mechanically transmit bacteria.
  • Ingestion of raw shellfish from sewage‑contaminated waters.
  • Direct contact with infected person or carrier.

Chronic Carrier State

  • Defined as excretion of Salmonella Typhi for >1 year.
  • More common in women, elderly, and patients with gallstones.
  • Gallbladder is the primary reservoir.
  • Carriers are asymptomatic but infectious.
  • Famous example: "Typhoid Mary" (Mary Mallon).

Prevention & Vaccination

Environmental & Personal

  • Safe drinking water (chlorination, boiling).
  • Proper sewage disposal and sanitation.
  • Handwashing before eating and after toilet.
  • Food safety: cook thoroughly, avoid raw shellfish, wash vegetables.
  • Fly control.

Vaccines Available

  • Typhoid conjugate vaccine (TCV) – single dose, recommended for children >6 months.
  • Vi polysaccharide vaccine (IM) – for adults and children >2 years.
  • Oral live attenuated Ty21a vaccine (capsules) – for adults/children >6 years.
  • Not 100% effective; still need hygiene.

Treatment & Complications

Antibiotic Therapy

  • First‑line: Ceftriaxone (IV/IM) or Azithromycin (oral).
  • Fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin) – high resistance; use only if sensitive.
  • Chloramphenicol – older drug; resistance common.
  • Duration: 7‑14 days.
  • Carrier state: treat with amoxicillin or ciprofloxacin for 4‑6 weeks; cholecystectomy if gallstones present.

Complications

  • Intestinal hemorrhage
  • Intestinal perforation (peritonitis – surgical emergency)
  • Toxic myocarditis
  • Encephalopathy
  • Relapse (5‑10%)

Prognosis

  • Excellent with prompt antibiotics.
  • Perforation increases mortality.
  • Chronic carrier state may persist despite treatment.

PHN Role & Nursing Care

  • Case reporting: Typhoid fever is a notifiable disease (under NESSS laboratory‑diagnosed).
  • Health education: Promote handwashing, safe water (boil/chlorinate), proper food handling.
  • Isolation: Enteric precautions for hospitalized patients (hand hygiene, disposal of feces/urine).
  • Contact tracing: Identify household contacts; educate on symptoms and hygiene.
  • Carrier detection: For food handlers, health workers, and household members of cases – stool culture after recovery.
  • Nursing care: Monitor vital signs (watch for relative bradycardia, signs of perforation/bleeding).
  • Fluid and nutrition: Encourage oral fluids; if severe, IV hydration.
  • Immunization promotion: Typhoid vaccine for high‑risk groups and travelers.
